Introduction to Cyber Security

What is Cyber Security?

Cybersecurity is the field of study and practice that responds to challenges as technology evolves. It is the practice of protecting systems, networks, and programs from digital attacks. These cyberattacks are usually aimed at accessing, changing, or destroying sensitive information; extorting money from users; or interrupting normal business processes.

> “Cybersecurity is the practice of protecting systems, networks, and programs from digital attacks.” \- cisco

Why Cyber Security?

Cyber security is important because government, military, corporate, financial, and medical organizations collect, process, and store unprecedented amounts of data on computers and other devices. A significant portion of that data can be sensitive information, whether that be intellectual property, financial data, personal information, or other types of data for which unauthorized access or exposure could have negative consequences.

CIA Triad

Confidentiality

Confidentiality is the assurance that the information is not disclosed to unauthorized individuals, processes, or devices. It is the most important aspect of the CIA triad.

This pillar of the triad refers to protecting private information from eyes that shouldn’t have access to it. Confidentiality is the need to enforce access - who can see this, and who shouldn’t? For example, we don’t want to give our social security number to just anyone, but we trust that the institutions we give them to - like tax services - implement the right security measures to keep it secret. So what tools are used to guarantee the right access?
#### Some of the ways confidentiality is managed are: - Keeping levels of access and setting permissions - Encrypting data and files - Using firewalls and intrusion detection systems - Requiring multi-factor authentication

Integrity

Integrity is the assurance that the information is trustworthy and accurate. It is the second most important aspect of the CIA triad.

This pillar of the triad refers to the trustworthiness of the information. Integrity is the need to ensure that the information is accurate and reliable. For example, we trust that the information we get from our bank is accurate and reliable. So what tools are used to guarantee the right information?
#### Some of the ways integrity is managed are: - Using digital signatures - Using checksums - Using access controls - Using change management

Availability

Availability is the assurance that the information is accessible to authorized users. It is the third most important aspect of the CIA triad.

This pillar of the triad refers to the accessibility of the information. Availability is the need to ensure that the information is accessible when needed. For example, we trust that the information we get from our bank is accessible when we need it. So what tools are used to guarantee the right access?
#### Some of the ways availability is managed are: - Using backups - Using redundant systems - Using disaster recovery plans - Using fault-tolerant systems

Cyber Threats

What is a Cyber Threat?

A cyber threat is a malicious act that seeks to damage data, steal data, or disrupt digital life in general. Cyber threats can come from within an organization by trusted users or from remote locations by unknown parties. Cyber threats can be intentional or unintentional.

### Types of Cyber Threats - Malware - Phishing - Sql injection - Cross Site Scripting - Denial of Service - Zero Day Exploitation

Malware

Malware is a type of software that is specifically designed to disrupt, damage, or gain unauthorized access to a computer system. Malware is a broad category that encompasses many different types of harmful software, including viruses, worms, trojans, ransomware, and spyware.

Phishing

Phishing is a type of social engineering attack often used to steal user data, including login credentials and credit card numbers. It occurs when an attacker, masquerading as a trusted entity, dupes a victim into opening an email, instant message, or text message. The recipient is then tricked into clicking a malicious link, which can lead to the installation of malware, the freezing of the system as part of a ransomware attack, or the revealing of sensitive information.

SQL Injection

SQL injection is a code injection technique that might destroy your database. SQL injection is one of the most common web hacking techniques. SQL injection is the placement of malicious code in SQL statements, via web page input.

Cross Site Scripting

Cross-site scripting (XSS) is a security bug that can affect websites. It allows an attacker to inject malicious scripts into web pages viewed by other users. The end user’s browser has no way to know that the script should not be trusted, and will execute the script. Because it thinks the script came from a trusted source, the malicious script can access any cookies, session tokens, or other sensitive information retained by the browser and used with that site. These scripts can even rewrite the content of the HTML page.

Denial of Service

A denial-of-service (DoS) attack is a cyber-attack in which the perpetrator seeks to make a machine or network resource unavailable to its intended users by temporarily or indefinitely disrupting services of a host connected to the Internet. Denial of service is typically accomplished by flooding the targeted machine or resource with superfluous requests in an attempt to overload systems and prevent some or all legitimate requests from being fulfilled.

Zero Day Exploitation

A zero-day (also known as 0-day) vulnerability is a computer-software vulnerability that is unknown to those who should be interested in mitigating the vulnerability (including the vendor of the target software). Until the vulnerability is mitigated, hackers can exploit it to adversely affect computer programs, data, additional computers or a network.

The Cyber Security Industry

Security Engineering

the technical implementation of various forms of security.

Information security, or InfoSec, protects data in any form from being accessed, modified, shared, or deleted by the wrong people.

Network security is the practice of securing a computer network infrastructure from unauthorized access.

Application security refers to implementing measures that defend an application (mobile, desktop, or web) from attack, including both software and hardware solutions. Examples of application security include secure coding, the use of antivirus programs, firewalls, and encryption.

Cloud security is the practice of protecting data stored online via cloud computing platforms from theft, leakage, and deletion.

Cryptography focuses on methods to hide and un-hide information so that data is only readable or usable by authorized people. This requires familiarity with all types of encryption and hashing algorithms.

Critical infrastructure security is defending physical systems that are becoming more digital/networked, such as energy grids, hospitals, water and waste systems, and even schools. Among the issues that come up are natural disasters and outages.

Red Team

In cybersecurity, a "red team" refers to a group of security professionals who are tasked with simulating attacks on a system, network, or organization. The goal of a red team is to identify and exploit vulnerabilities in a manner that mimics the tactics, techniques, and procedures (TTPs) of real-world attackers. This is done to assess the security posture of a system or organization and to help improve its defenses.

Red Teaming

Red Teaming refers to the practice of conducting simulated attacks or adversarial exercises to test the security defenses of a system, network, or organization. The red team, in this context, is the group of professionals responsible for carrying out these simulations. They mimic the actions of potential attackers to identify vulnerabilities and weaknesses in the organization's security measures.

Red Team Leader: Responsible for overseeing the entire red team engagement, coordinating efforts, and ensuring the team stays focused on the objectives.

Adversarial Emulation Specialist: Simulates the tactics, techniques, and procedures (TTPs) of real-world adversaries. This role may involve ethical hacking, penetration testing, and other offensive security activities.

SOC (Security Operations Center) Analyst: Simulates the response of the internal security team to the red team's actions. This helps evaluate the effectiveness of the organization's incident detection and response capabilities.

Physical Security Specialist: Focuses on assessing and testing the physical security measures in place, such as access controls, surveillance systems, and security personnel responses.

Social Engineering Specialist: Conducts phishing attacks, phone-based attacks, or other forms of social engineering to test the organization's resilience to manipulative tactics.

Network Security Specialist: Evaluates the organization's network infrastructure, identifying vulnerabilities and weaknesses in the network architecture, firewalls, and other security devices.

Application Security Specialist: Examines the security of applications, both web-based and local, looking for vulnerabilities and potential exploits.

Wireless Security Specialist: Assesses the security of wireless networks and devices, including Wi-Fi and Bluetooth, to identify potential points of compromise.

Insider Threat Specialist: Simulates the actions of an insider threat, assessing the organization's ability to detect and mitigate internal risks.

Open Source Intelligence (OSINT) Analyst: Gathers information from publicly available sources to simulate the way adversaries might collect intelligence about the organization.

Physical Penetration Tester: Focuses on testing the physical security of buildings and facilities, attempting to gain unauthorized access through methods like lock picking or tailgating.

Exploit Developer: Develops or leverages exploits to demonstrate the impact of vulnerabilities, showing the potential consequences if a real attacker were to exploit them.

Malware Developer: individual who specializes in creating or customizing malicious software, commonly known as malware, as part of offensive security activities. The role of a malware developer on a red team is to simulate the tactics, techniques, and procedures (TTPs) of real-world adversaries by crafting or modifying malware to assess an organization's security posture.

Blue Team

In cybersecurity, a "blue team" refers to a group of security professionals who are tasked with defending an organization's systems, networks, and data from cyber threats. The goal of a blue team is to identify and mitigate vulnerabilities, monitor for potential threats, and respond to security incidents. This is done to improve the overall security posture of the organization and protect against cyber attacks.

Blue Teaming

Blue Teaming refers to the practice of defending an organization's systems, networks, and data from cyber threats. The blue team, in this context, is the group of professionals responsible for implementing and maintaining security measures to protect the organization's assets. They work to identify and mitigate vulnerabilities, monitor for potential threats, and respond to security incidents.

Security Analyst: Responsible for monitoring and analyzing security events and alerts to identify potential threats and vulnerabilities.

Incident Responder: Responds to security incidents, investigates potential breaches, and coordinates the organization's response to cyber threats.

Security Engineer: Designs, implements, and maintains security controls and technologies to protect the organization's systems and data.

Threat Hunter: Proactively searches for signs of potential threats and vulnerabilities within the organization's systems and networks.

Forensic Analyst: Investigates security incidents and breaches, collects and analyzes digital evidence, and provides support for legal and law enforcement investigations.

Security Operations Center (SOC) Analyst: Monitors and analyzes security events and alerts, responds to potential threats, and escalates incidents as needed.

Security Architect: Designs and implements security solutions and architectures to protect the organization's systems and data.

Security Administrator: Manages and maintains security controls and technologies, including firewalls, intrusion detection systems, and access controls.

Ways to Get a Job in Cyber Security

Education

Many cybersecurity jobs require a bachelor's degree in computer science, information technology, or a related field. Some positions may require a master's degree or other advanced certifications. A strong educational background can provide the foundational knowledge and skills needed to succeed in the field.

CTF & Bug Bounty

CTF

CTF stands for "Capture The Flag," and in the context of cybersecurity, it refers to a type of competition or exercise where participants engage in various challenges to solve security-related problems. The goal is to find and exploit vulnerabilities, analyze code, decrypt messages, or perform other tasks to obtain flags hidden within the systems.

Binary Exploitation/Reverse Engineering

Participants analyze and manipulate binary files, often involving reverse engineering to understand and exploit software vulnerabilities.
